My Drone Building Process!

This page is all about my drone building process — including troubleshooting!

Tools You Need

First, let's talk about the extra tools you’ll need. One of the most important things is a soldering iron and some solder. You can get a decent one for around $30–$50 (not including solder).

I personally use the Pinecil soldering iron, which I found very helpful. It costs about $40 and is easy to use. I highly recommend it if you're just starting out with building drones.

You also need a few basic tools like an allen key, a wrench, and a screwdriver. Zipties are very useful for organizing cables and securing components, and wire cutters are essential too.

Frame

I chose the Phistal Mark5 Frame. It’s a 5-inch frame with nice 3D-printed mounts for the GPS, antenna, and buzzer. It’s a very decent frame, but I made the mistake of getting the DeadCat version. Since I’m used to it, it was okay, but if you’re starting out, I would recommend the normal X-frame.

Motors

For motors, I went with the EMAX ECO II 2207 motors. These are 2207 motors, which means they have a stator diameter of 22mm and a stator height of 7mm. They are very powerful and can handle up to 6S batteries, which is great for racing drones.

I found them to be very responsive and efficient, making them a great choice for my build. They come with lock nuts that must be tightened with a wrench — I made the mistake of tightening them by hand, and they came loose during flight, causing a crash. Make sure to use a wrench to secure them properly!

When buying, choose the 2400KV version for 5-inch drones, as the 1700KV version is more suitable for 6-inch drones.

ESC and Flight Controller Stack

This part can be hit or miss depending on your experience and the quality of the components you choose. Instead of going in blindly, I recommend doing your research and choosing components that fit your budget and skill level. Watching a Joshua Bardwell video is very helpful.

For my build, I went with the Speedy Bee F405 Stack. It’s a 4-in-1 ESC and flight controller stack that's reliable for many people while remaining very affordable.

The motors are wired to the ESC with three wires going to three corresponding pads. The XT60 connector's black wire goes to the negative terminal and the red wire goes to the positive terminal.

The flight controller and ESC are connected via an 8-pin connector.

Camera and VTX

The camera and VTX are not too complicated to install. The VTX usually has four wires: VTX, VCC, GND, and TX, which go to their respective pads. The exception is TX — it goes to the RX pad. Think of it like this: the TX pad is transmitting information, and the RX pad is receiving it. You can’t connect two receiving pads together, or else no information will be exchanged.

The camera has three wires: VCC (red), GND (black), and CAM (yellow or white). These go to their respective pads with no changes required.

I used the Caddx Ant Camera and the TBS Unify Pro VTX.

ELRS Receiver and Antenna

For the receiver, I went with the BetaFlight ELRS Receiver. It’s a very affordable receiver. The receiver has 4 wires: VCC red, GND black, TX yellow, and RX white. The wiring diagram follows the same logic as the VTX — TX to RX and vice versa, and VCC and GND to the respective pads.

The VTX antenna is fairly simple except for one thing: the VTX antenna has to be the same polarization as your goggles' antenna. If you have a RHCP (right-hand circular polarization) antenna on your goggles, you need a RHCP antenna on your VTX. If you have a LHCP (left-hand circular polarization) antenna on your goggles, you need a LHCP antenna on your VTX. If you mix and match, you will experience significant signal loss and poor video quality. Also, the connector type of the antenna must match the connector type of the VTX. The most common connector types are SMA and RP-SMA, so make sure to check which one your VTX uses before purchasing an antenna.

GPS

The GPS is not a necessary component for a 5-inch FPV drone, but it can be useful for certain features like return-to-home and position hold. If you choose to include a GPS module in your build, make sure to select one that is compatible with your flight controller and has good reviews from other users. The GPS module typically connects to the flight controller via a dedicated port or through the UART interface, so be sure to follow the wiring instructions provided by the manufacturer. The GPS module should be mounted in a location that has a clear view of the sky to ensure good satellite reception. Common mounting locations include the top of the drone or on a dedicated GPS mast. Keep in mind that adding a GPS module will increase the weight of your drone, which may affect its flight performance, so it's important to consider this when designing your build. The GPS module that I used is the HGLRC M100 Pro GPS Module.
